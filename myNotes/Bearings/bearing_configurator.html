1. What is a Bearing?
Definition & Purpose

A bearing is a machine element that constrains relative motion between two parts — typically a rotating shaft and a stationary housing — to a desired type (rotation, linear, etc.) while reducing friction and transferring loads. Without bearings, surfaces in contact would wear out rapidly due to sliding friction.

Primary Function
Reduce Friction
between moving parts
Secondary Function
Support Loads
radial, axial, or both
Tertiary Function
Guide Motion

2. Load Types on Bearings
Radial Load

Acts perpendicular to the shaft axis. Example: weight of a pulley sitting on a shaft. All deep groove ball bearings, cylindrical roller bearings are designed primarily for this.

Fr → perpendicular to shaft axis
P = Fr / (2·r·l)  [projected pressure]
Axial (Thrust) Load

Acts along the shaft axis. Example: force on a helical gear, propeller thrust. Thrust ball bearings handle pure axial. Angular contact bearings handle combined loads.

Fa → parallel to shaft axis
Combined: Pe = X·V·Fr + Y·Fa

4. Anatomy of a Rolling Element Bearing
Components
  • Inner ring (IR): Press-fitted on shaft. Rotates with shaft.
  • Outer ring (OR): Fits in housing. Usually stationary.
  • Rolling elements: Balls or rollers. Transfer load between rings via rolling contact.
  • Cage (retainer): Keeps rolling elements evenly spaced. Prevents contact between them.
  • Seals / shields: Retain lubricant, exclude contaminants. Suffix -2RS (rubber seal), -2Z (metal shield).
  • Lubricant: Grease or oil. Reduces contact stress, removes heat.
Contact Geometry

Ball bearings: Point contact → small contact patch → lower load capacity but lower friction and higher speed.

Roller bearings: Line contact → larger contact area → higher load capacity but more friction. Cylindrical, tapered, spherical, needle — all are line contact variants.

Hertz contact stress (ball): σmax = 0.578 × (F·E*²/R*²)^(1/3)
Life exponent: a = 3 (ball)  |  a = 10/3 (roller)
5. Bearing Life — L₁₀ Theory (Lundberg–Palmgren)
Fatigue Life & Dynamic Load Rating

Bearings fail primarily by rolling contact fatigue — subsurface cracks that propagate until pitting/spalling occurs. Lundberg & Palmgren (1947) derived a statistical model relating load, life, and material.

L₁₀ = (C / Pe)^a  ×  10⁶ revolutions

C  = Dynamic load rating [kN] — load at which 90% of bearings survive 10⁶ rev
Pe = Equivalent dynamic load [kN] = X·V·Fr + Y·Fa
a  = 3 for ball bearings, 10/3 for roller bearings
L₁₀(hours) = (10⁶ / 60n) × L₁₀(rev)

Key insight: Doubling the load reduces life by a factor of 8 (ball) or ~10.6 (roller). Halving the load multiplies life by 8× — so reducing load is extremely effective.

Required Rating C₁₀
C₁₀ = Pe × (L_D/L_R)^(1/a)
minimum catalog C needed

6. Reliability & Weibull Statistics
Beyond L₁₀ — Adjusting for Reliability

L₁₀ corresponds to 90% reliability (10% failure probability). For critical applications requiring higher reliability, a Weibull adjustment factor aR is applied:

LR = aR × L₁₀

Weibull CDF: R(x) = exp[−((x − x₀)/(θ − x₀))^b]
x₀ = 0.02,  θ − x₀ = 4.439,  b = 1.483  (SKF standard)

R = 99% → aR ≈ 0.21  |  R = 95% → aR ≈ 0.62  |  R = 90% → aR = 1.0

Note: Going from 90% to 99% reliability cuts the expected life to ~21% of L₁₀. This is why high-reliability applications (aerospace, nuclear) specify very conservative bearing loads.

7. Journal Bearing Theory — Hydrodynamic Lubrication
Petroff's Law (lightly loaded)

When a shaft rotates in a lubricated bore, it drags oil around, building up a hydrodynamic wedge that lifts the shaft off the bore surface. Petroff's equation gives friction for the concentric (lightly loaded) case:

f = 2π² × (μN/P) × (r/c)

μ = absolute viscosity [Pa·s]
N = shaft speed [rev/s]
P = W/(2rl) — projected pressure [Pa]
r/c = radius-to-clearance ratio
Sommerfeld Number

The Sommerfeld number S is the key dimensionless parameter governing journal bearing performance. It determines eccentricity ratio, minimum film thickness, and all performance characteristics:

S = (r/c)² × (μN/P)

High S → concentric shaft, thick film, low eccentricity
Low S  → eccentric shaft, thin film, risk of contact

Stable thick-film: μN/P ≥ 1.7 × 10⁻⁶
8. Lubrication Regimes — Stribeck Curve
Three Regimes of Lubrication
Boundary Lubrication
μN/P → Low
Surfaces in contact. High wear. Additive-dependent. Start/stop conditions.
Mixed Lubrication
Transition
Partial film. Some asperity contact. Moderate wear. Unstable regime — avoid sustained operation.
Hydrodynamic (Full Film)
μN/P → High
Complete oil film separation. Near-zero wear. Desired operating condition.

The Stribeck curve plots friction coefficient vs. the bearing characteristic number (μN/P). Minimum friction occurs at the transition from mixed to hydrodynamic. Operating well into the hydrodynamic regime is the design goal for journal bearings.

10. Bearing Fits & Tolerances
Shaft Fits (Inner Ring)

The inner ring must not creep on the shaft under rotating load. A transition or interference fit is used:

  • k5 / m5: Light interference — small motors, light load
  • n6 / p6: Medium interference — standard industrial
  • r6 / s6: Heavy interference — heavy shock load

Interference expands bore slightly — reduces internal clearance (C3 suffix compensates).

Housing Fits (Outer Ring)

Outer ring is usually stationary — a clearance or transition fit allows easy replacement:

  • H7: Standard — allows axial float (floating bearing)
  • J7 / K7: Transition — light press for fixed end
  • M7 / N7: Interference — heavy load, non-separable housing

Fixed + floating arrangement: one end fixed (axial location), other end free to slide thermally.

11. Lubrication — Grease vs. Oil
Selection Guide

Grease Lubrication

  • Used in ~80–90% of rolling bearings
  • Simple — sealed for life possible (-2RS bearings)
  • Speed limit: n·dm ≤ 300,000 mm/min typically
  • Relubrication interval from manufacturer charts
  • NLGI Grade 2 most common (medium stiffness)

Oil Lubrication

  • Required for high speed, high temperature, or heat removal
  • Oil bath, circulating, oil mist, or jet systems
  • ISO VG 32–220 typical for rolling bearings
  • Viscosity must be adequate at operating temperature
  • Mandatory for journal (plain) bearings — full film needed

13. Quick Selection Rules of Thumb
Speed Priority

High speed → Deep groove ball or angular contact ball. Roller bearings have higher friction at speed. Check speed rating (n × dm limit).

Load Priority

Heavy load → Cylindrical roller (radial), Spherical roller (combined + misalignment), Tapered roller (heavy combined). C rating ~30–50% higher than ball for same size.

Misalignment

Shaft deflection or housing bore misalignment → Self-aligning ball (light load) or Spherical roller (heavy load). Both tolerate 1–3° misalignment.
