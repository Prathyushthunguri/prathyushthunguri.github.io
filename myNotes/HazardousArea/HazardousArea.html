Hazardous Area Classification — Zone Definitions

Per EN 60079-10 / DSEAR 2002 / ATEX Directive. Area classification deals with reasonably foreseeable events only — catastrophic failures (vessel rupture, unpredictable events) are outside scope and require separate risk assessment.

Gas / Vapour / Mist Zones (EN 60079-10)

0
Continuous Hazard
Explosive atmosphere from gas, vapour, mist or spray is present continuously, or for long periods, or frequently.
e.g. Inside solvent storage tank operating continuously above flash point
> 1000 hrs/yr
1
Occasional Hazard
Explosive atmosphere is likely to occur in normal operation occasionally.
e.g. Sampling point where liquid is above flash point temperature
10–1000 hrs/yr
2
Rare / Short Duration
Explosive atmosphere not likely in normal operation but if it does occur, persists for a short period only.
e.g. Leakage from gas pipework flanges, fittings
< 10 hrs/yr

Combustible Dust Zones (ATEX / IEC 61241)

20
Continuous Dust Cloud
Explosive atmosphere as a cloud of combustible dust in air present continuously, or for long periods, or frequently.
e.g. Inside cyclones, hoppers, containers, pipework, grinding equipment
> 1000 hrs/yr
21
Occasional Dust Cloud
Explosive dust cloud atmosphere likely to occur in normal operation occasionally, or where dust layers likely to give rise to explosive concentration.
e.g. Powder filling/discharge points, weigh stations, sampling points
10–1000 hrs/yr
22
Rare Dust Cloud
Explosive dust cloud atmosphere not likely in normal operation but if it does occur, persists for a short period only.
e.g. Outlets from cyclones, clean-side of dust filters
< 10 hrs/yr

⚠️ Catastrophic failures (vessel rupture, unpredictable events) are outside the scope of area classification per EN 60079-10 §1.1(d). Such events may cause explosive atmospheres in areas classified as non-hazardous and require risk assessment under separate legislation.

Dust Layer Risks

⚡ Risk 1 — Secondary Explosion
A primary explosion within a building may raise dust layers into clouds, causing secondary explosions more damaging than the primary event. Always control dust layers.
🔥 Risk 2 — Layer Fire
Dust layers may be ignited by heat flux from equipment on which the layer rests. Risk is of fire rather than explosion — this may be a slow process.
💥 Risk 3 — Cloud Ignition on Hot Surface
Dust layer raised into cloud may ignite on a hot surface causing explosion. Note: cloud ignition temperatures often much higher than layer temperatures (e.g. Paper: layer 210–275°C, cloud >450°C).
Dust layer rule: A 1mm deposit in a 10×10×5m room = ~75 kg powder. If dispersed → 150 g/m³ (well within flammable range). Never use air-jetting to clean dust deposits. Equipment surface layers must not exceed 5 mm.

Particle Size Guidance

Particles >500 µm are unlikely to cause dust explosion hazards. Most tests are carried out below 100 µm or standardised 63 µm. Note: transportation of granular material may create a fines fraction through attrition — test the finest, driest material from the actual process.

Ignition Source Reference

⚡ Mechanical Sparks
Arise when two hardened materials contact with force — glancing impact and friction sparks. Thermite reaction (Al, Ti, Mg, Zr with rusty steel) produces highly incendive white-hot sparks capable of igniting most flammable gases, vapours, mists and dusts.
✔ Control: Maintain circumferential contact velocity below 1 m/s. Use pneumatic separators to remove debris upstream of mills.
🌡 Hot Surfaces
Binding friction (screw conveyors, mills), drying operations, hot particles from exhausts. Dryer inlet/outlet temperatures must be restricted. Surface layers must not exceed 5mm. Category-dependent max surface temperature limits apply (see Equipment tab).
✔ Control: 50K below MIT cloud; 10–20K below Air-Over-Layer exotherm onset. Clean burners, fit 3mm mesh on inlets, clean/filter recirculated air.
🔥 Flames & Hot Gases
Unauthorised hot work is the most obvious risk. Smouldering material inside plant may cause ignition on start-up. Burning embers from truck engines in bulk storage areas. Hot work is a well-known cause of dust explosions.
✔ Control: Strict hot-work permit system. Certified flame arrester on bulk storage truck access. Fit flame traps on all vents handling flammable gases/vapours.
⚡ Electrical Apparatus
Must be ATEX certified for the correct Gas Group and Temperature Class (T-rating). Correct Ingress Protection (IP) rating essential — typically IP5X or IP6X for dust. Clean more than daily → replace or relocate if not IP6X.
✔ Control: ATEX certification to match zone. Select T-rating <2/3 MITcloud AND >75K below MIT5mm layer. Use correct IP rating.
⚡ Static Electricity
Contact electrification at interfaces — fluid flow in pipes, drum charging, walking on floors, pouring powders, spraying. Insulating materials (PTFE, poly-tubs, low-conductivity liquids like Toluene, n-Hexane) create highest charging currents. Brush discharges up to 4 mJ from charged insulating surfaces.
✔ Control: Earth ALL metal items — resistance <10Ω. Charge-dissipative footwear/flooring where MIE <30 mJ. Restrict insulating plastic surface area (see tab). Type C or D FIBCs only.
⚡ Lightning
If lightning strikes a flammable atmosphere, ignition will always occur. High induced voltages from thunderstorms can also affect equipment even without direct strikes. Large currents from strike point can produce sparks nearby.
✔ Control: Consult specialist for site-specific risk. Fit suitable flame traps on all vents. Bond and earth all structures.
🔬 Chemical Reactions / Thermal Instability
Auto-ignition in dryers/furnaces. Pyrophoric metal powders and catalysts. Smouldering deposits in dryers and grinders. Auto-oxidation of fibrous insulation contaminated with oil at elevated temperatures. Some materials self-heat from ~130°C with peak >700°C.
✔ Control: Identify hot dry material accumulation areas. Use closed-cell insulation near oil systems. Remove contaminated lagging only under HOT WORK permit.
⚡ Electrostatic Discharge Types
Spark: Between two conductors (full plasma channel). Corona: Point discharge, no plasma channel. Brush: Charged non-conductor → earthed conductor (up to 4 mJ). Propagating Brush: Polarised insulating sheets — very high energy. Cone: Highly charged, high-resistivity granular materials.
✔ Most hazardous: Propagating brush discharge (requires Type A bags to be avoided). Restrict insulating surface areas. All metal items earthed.

The 'T' rating is the maximum surface temperature attained by equipment under the most adverse operating conditions (within recognised tolerance). Used to ensure no hot surface can ignite the surrounding explosive atmosphere.

Dryer Inlet/Outlet Temperature Limits

For explosion-protected dryers: Inlet/outlet temperatures must be restricted to: 50K below MIT cloud value AND 10–20K below Air-Over-Layer exotherm onset temperature. Hot particles from direct-fired systems must be eliminated (clean/filtered recirculated air; clean burners; 3mm mesh on air inlet).

Section 6 — Prevention Strategies

The question is: can we, under all normal and abnormal operations, eliminate one of the THREE basic components of combustion — Fuel, Oxidant, or Ignition Source?

🔴 1. Inherent Safety (Preferred)
Eliminate the hazard entirely. Per Trevor Kletz: "You cannot fall down stairs you don't have." Substitute flammable solvent with non-flammable alternative. Change process to avoid hazard formation. Reduce quantity of dangerous substances to minimum.
🟡 2. Prevention of Atmosphere
Dilution: Add water to alcohol — raises flash point. At 25°C with 5K margin, IPA/H₂O can be ≤15% v/v IPA (FP=30°C). Methanol/water: up to ~40% v/v at 25°C.

Inerting: Maintain O₂ below MOC/LOC. Safety factor 2–3% below limiting value. Design basis max 5% O₂; shutdown at 7.5% O₂ (for Toluene/N₂: MOC=9.5%).
🟢 3. Ventilation / Extraction
Dilute airborne concentrations below LEL/MEC. Ductwork concentration max 25% MEC (10–100 g/m³). Monitor airflow — fit LF alarm + LLF trip. Smoke/bubble tests to verify airflow effectiveness at point of interest. Enables Zone 2 NE (negligible extent) designation in boiler houses, battery charging areas, etc.

Flash Point / Flammable Atmosphere Formation

Safety Margins for Flash Point
  • Pure solvents: 5K safety margin above flash point
  • Liquid/liquid or liquid/solid mixtures: 15K safety margin recommended
  • Spillages: Use open-cup flash point value (not closed-cup)
  • High shear operations (mixing, splash loading) may produce non-equilibrium conditions — flammable mists well below flash point
  • Fine mists can form flammable mixtures 200K or more below flash point
IPA / Water Flash Point Formula
Empirical relationship (Nugentec data):

y = 106.7 × x−0.4674

where y = flash point (°C), x = IPA concentration (% v/v)

At 25°C ambient + 5K margin → max 15% IPA v/v (FP≈30°C) to avoid flammable atmosphere.

Section 7 — Explosion Protection Methods

Protection assumes ignition and explosion may occur and mitigates the effects. Options depend on vessel/layout and material characteristics. ANY protection system must cover upstream and downstream interconnections.

Design Options

EPRD — Explosion Pressure Resistant Design
No deformation of vessel permitted. Must withstand full Pmax (typically 8–10 bar.g for dusts; 6–8 bar.g for common gases/hydrocarbons). Less common due to high design strength required.
EPSRD — Explosion Pressure Shock Resistant Design
Deformation is acceptable, but not total failure. Allows vessel to absorb some explosion energy through plastic deformation.

Protection Methods Comparison

MethodHow It WorksLoss of Containment?Key Considerations
Explosion Venting Burst panel/vent opens when Pstat reached. Relieves pressure to safe area. Pred,max must not exceed vessel design pressure. Yes — process material ejected Vent duct increases back-pressure. Recoil force on vessel. Unburned dust ejected ahead of fireball. Sensors on vent panel for auto-shutdown. Explosion Suppression Detects incipient explosion (pressure/UV/IR). Injects suppressant into growing fireball within milliseconds to quench flame. No — containment maintained Suppressant quantity depends on Kst, Pred, vessel geometry and design pressure. Requires detection/control system. Explosion Pressure Containment Vessel designed to withstand full Pmax with no relief. No — full containment Less common. Very high design strength needed (8–10 bar.g for dusts). Suitable for some milling operations.
Isolation / Decoupling Prevents propagation of flame, pressure and burning particles between interconnected equipment. Depends on method Options: ATEX rotary valve, rapid-action slam-shut valve, chemical barrier, product choke. Per prEN 15089. All interconnections must be covered.

Isolation Device Options

Rotary Valve
ATEX certified. Provides continuous isolation. Wear rates and blade-to-casing gap tolerances must be maintained. Periodic inspection critical. Often contentious due to ongoing wear requirements.
Rapid-Action (Slam-Shut) Valve
Active or passive. Design pressure applied up to isolation device — all ducting to this point must withstand Pred or Pmax. Closure time + detection response time + flame speed determines minimum distance L from explosion source (typically L >5000 mm).
Chemical Barrier / Product Choke
Chemical extinguishing barrier suppresses flame propagation. Product choke uses material column to prevent flame travel. Simpler mechanically but requires careful design for specific material and flow conditions.
Critical reminder: Venting does NOT prevent an explosion — it only limits explosion pressure. Flame, pressure effects, and flying debris outside the enclosure must be anticipated. Equipment must shut down automatically on explosion detection to prevent burning material transfer.
