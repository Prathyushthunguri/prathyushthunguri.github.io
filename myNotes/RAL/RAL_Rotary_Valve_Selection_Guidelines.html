What is a Rotary Valve?

  • Compact device for continuous discharge of bulk powders / granules under gravity flow
  • Single moving part — multi-vane Rotor revolving with close clearances in housing
  • Housing: inlet at top, outlet at bottom

Functional Uses

①Control product flow rate (fixed or variable)
②Maintain gas pressure differential — airlock
③Act as explosion barrier
④Act as flame barrier
⑤Act as process isolator / barrier

Limitations

Rotary Valve does NOT create product flow — only passes what can fall under gravity
All Rotary Valves leak air/gas even with no pressure differential
Faster rotor speed → lower & more erratic filling efficiency
Greater pressure differential → less predictable filling efficiency

1. Basic Performance — Rotor Speed

  • Max Rotor Tip Speed: 40 m/min — above this, increase in speed reduces throughput
  • Why: gravity-fed product must accelerate from rest & fill pockets before rotating away from inlet. Too fast → incomplete filling → throughput drops

May be LOWER if:

  • Low bulk density product — light materials take longer to settle; won't fill pockets fully at high speed
  • Significant pressure differential opposing product flow — upward airflow pushes product back; aeration worsens filling

May be HIGHER if:

  • Fed under cascade flow (screw feeders, belt feeders, weighers) — material enters more consistently; rotor doesn't wait for gravity

Normal Practice:

Rotor Diameter Speed Limit
Up to 300 mmBelow 25 RPM
Above 300 mmBelow 40 m/min tip speed
150 mm (6") rotorMax ~85 RPM (theoretical)
1000 mm (39.3") rotorMax ~12.7 RPM

2. Product Physical Characteristics

  • Dictates detailed design & construction of individual components
  • For abrasive materials entrained by high velocity leakage air → erosion of surfaces: lower pressure differential is better
  • Challenging characteristics to handle: sticky, abrasive, corrosive, biologically sensitive, cohesive, toxic, flammable, explosive, non-friable — often in combination

3. System / Duty Requirements

Physical Installation

  • Flange details: Square / Round / Combination
  • Type of drive: Chain or Direct coupled
  • Inbuilt venting: present or not

Pressure Differential

  • Higher ΔP → greater air leakage
  • High upward leakage causes:
    • Reduced material flow by reducing bulk density (fluidisation)
    • Physically opposing product flow
    • Enhanced abrasiveness of hard fine particles
    • Moisture transfer & unstable flow conditions

Operating Clearances & Temperature

Material Nominal Clearance Reason
Cast Iron / Carbon Steel 0.13 mm Minimal leakage at ambient
Stainless Steel 0.18 mm Minimise galling/seizing risk on rotor-housing contact
  • Operating temp >30°C above ambient → clearances need increasing
  • Rotor is lighter → heats faster than Housing → different expansion rates during warm-up

4. Individual Industry Needs

General / Quarrying

  • Performance & durability primary concern
  • Standard construction acceptable

Food & Pharmaceutical

  • Demanding in design detail, surface finish
  • cGMP manufacturing required
  • Extensive documentation, certification, FAT

5. Legislation

  • Machinery Directive — applies to all
  • PED (Pressure Equipment Directive) — mainly affects valves ≥450 mm; hydrostatic testing required
  • ATEX 94/9/EC — can affect design & construction for explosive atmospheres

Housing Body

  • Normally cast construction for rigidity & cost
  • Fabrications used for non-standard configurations & large one-off valves
  • Fabricated / machined-from-solid increasingly used for pharmaceutical (flaw-free validation)

Inlet Types

Inline Inlets
  • Classic configuration
  • Practical for most applications
Offset Inlets
  • Ensures valve never achieves 100% filling efficiency
  • Minimises incident of product shearing
  • Some have 'plough' profile to disperse product away from shear point

End Covers

Inboard Mounted Bearings

  • Low cost, low duty valves
  • Low pressure differential applications
  • Filter/cyclone & general discharge duties
  • Product must not have difficult handling characteristics

Outboard / Outrigger Extensions

  • Limited protection from product contamination (seal failure → product jets at bearing)
  • Primary reason: Space for wide range of shaft sealing arrangements
  • Larger to accommodate bigger shaft diameters — limits rotor deflection under high loads

Air Purge Connections

  • Sequentially pulsed or continuous — dislodge hanging product at valve outlet
  • Can pressurise void between End Cover & End Discs when using Closed Rotors — prevents product entry

Blowing Seals (Blow-Through Models)

Direct Purge

  • True blow-through of Rotor Pocket
  • Most efficient; wider range of materials
  • Restricts drive arrangements — pipe connections close to rotor shaft

Semi-Direct Purge

  • Entrainment trough — air passes partly along trough, partly through rotor
  • More common due to fewer drive constraints

Entrainment Boxes

  • When blow-through not suitable — normal drop-through RVs used with separate Transition Box to connect to blowline
  • Adaptable for different pipe sizes; preferred for some products
  • Best design: Simple straight-sided, tapering down to pipe — deeper the better

Rotor Types

✓ Open Vane (Preferred)

  • Better performance
  • Adaptable with special features
  • More tolerant to product changes
  • Can be purged if necessary
  • Compatible with Blowing Seals

⚠ Closed Vane

  • NOT suitable for Blowing Seals
  • Problems: product enters void between End Cover & Rotor disc; high wear in bore; product hang-up risk
  • Use only when: heavy shearing unavoidable; squealing from product build-up on housing
  • Pros: more vane support & rigidity
Typical: 8 pockets → minimum 4 vanes sealing at any time. More vanes = better sealing, but lower filling efficiency.

Replaceable Blades

Adjustable on assembly. Economic way to introduce alternative MOC for duty requirements. Note: NOT intended to compensate for wear (wear is uneven & not confined to blades).

Hardened / Alloy / Hard-faced Steel

  • For abrasion resistance

Scraper

  • Controls product build-up on Housing bore & End Cover faces

Flexible (PU / Natural Rubber)

  • Fibrous products or products containing large non-friable particles

Plastic

  • Non-stick / product compatible

Bronze / Bronze-Tipped

  • Ultra close clearances

Sticky Product — Hang-up Solutions

  • Scalloped pockets — standard approach for sticky materials
  • Non-stick coating — PTFE, Zylon (subject to wear; ok if no contamination concern)
  • Stainless Steel + polished surfaces — more expensive but suitable when contamination is issue
  • Air purging — lateral pulses injected at End Covers for extreme cases

Helical Vanes / Staggered Pockets

  • Purpose: smooth out pulsed discharge from valve
  • Standard rotor discharges bulk of product within 10% of pocket exposure → pulsed output
  • Use when pulsed output is a problem
  • Only for Fixed Vane Rotors

Shaft Seal Types

Felt

  • Very low cost, low duty only

Lip Seals

  • Standard: replaces or supports Felt seals
  • Specialised FDA-approved versions for high-end food & pharma

Gland Packing ★ Most Common

  • Low cost, versatile, replaceable in-situ
  • Various woven fibres + lubricant
  • General, food & pharma use
  • With Carbon fibres: up to 1000°C+

Pressurised Seals

  • Double seal; void pressurised above max working pressure
  • Leaks inward on seal failure
  • Failure detectable via pressure/flow sensors

Lantern Ring Air Purge

  • Positive inward air/gas flow → keeps product out of seal area
  • Recommended: fine abrasive products, 'searching' materials
  • Warning: loss of air supply when running → blocked passages

Labyrinth Scroll Air Purge

  • Purge air through scrolled channel
  • Mechanically screws ingressing product back into valve
  • Best for fine, highly abrasive products

Mechanical Seals

  • Most products, especially fine abrasive
  • Normally used with air purge
  • Expensive

CIP Seals

  • Wet cleaning & drying in-place
  • Food & pharma specific
  • Integral with End Cover — bespoke valves only

Bearings

  • Standard: sealed-for-life deep groove ball race — grease filled, neoprene/similar integral seals
  • Standard ratings: up to 200°C; alternative materials, shields & lubricants extend to 800°C; plain bearings above 800°C
  • Size dictated by rotor shaft diameter (not pure load/speed) → typically oversized → near-infinite life in clean conditions
  • Vulnerable to product contamination — shaft seals must be checked & maintained regularly
  • Mounted directly in End Cover for concentricity control; cartridge/pillow block bearings lack finesse for close-clearance valves
  • Very large valves: cartridge assemblies may be used to avoid removing heavy End Covers for bearing replacement

Drive Arrangements

Chain & Sprocket Drive ★ Most Popular
  • Easy on-site speed change → calibrates throughput
  • Chain rated against breaking strain → acts as crude overload protection
  • Limited space for sprocket on smaller Blowing Seals
Direct Drive
  • Preferred for valves requiring regular dismantling for internal cleaning
  • Facilitates easy withdrawal of both End Covers
All Rotary Valves driven via reduction gearbox + electric induction motor. Hydraulic motors also used in some cases.

Accessories

Positive Overload Protection

  • Shear pin or proprietary torque limiting devices
  • Costly; subject to abuse — often omitted in favour of chain breakage

Non-Rotation Detection

  • Detects rotor stoppage

Speed Monitors

  • Monitors rotor speed in real time

Safety Switches

  • Fitted on Easy-Clean valves

Variable Speed Controls

  • Allows feed rate adjustment; ensure speed doesn't exceed ATEX limits

Air Purge Equipment

  • Pressure regulators & flow meters for Air Purge Seals; rotor pocket purging equipment

RotaSafe RM — Rotor Contact Detection

  • Intrinsically safe instant detection of rotor-to-housing contact
  • Automatically cuts power to drive motor on contact

Benefits

  • Protects valve from internal damage
  • Eliminates metal shedding contamination in product
  • Prevents start-up if valve reassembled incorrectly
  • Detects bearing failure
  • Protects ATEX 'autonomous safety system' certification — internal surface damage immediately renders valve unsafe

Food Applications — Overview

Product Type Risk Level Construction Requirement
Sugar, salt, chocolate (sterilising/preserving agents) Low Standard valve construction; StSt preferred as good practice
Flour, rice (long storage life if dry; further cooked) Low–Medium Standard; StSt preferred
Biodegradable food products Medium StSt essential; crack/crevice free; welds & surfaces polished Ra 0.8 or better; USDA/FDA approved materials; easy access for cleaning
Baby food, high fat milk powder High Mirror finish; support rails for safe extraction; regular cleaning required

Food — Cleaning Access Options

'Easy-Clean' — Quick Release Rotor
  • Low cost option
  • Components can be easily damaged
  • Not operator-friendly for larger sizes — weight & sharp edges are hazards
  • Requires risk assessment under Safe Handling regulations
'Fast Clean' — Support Rails
  • Safe & easy extraction via support rails
  • Access to both ends of valve
  • No additional handling equipment required
  • Recommended for high-sensitivity materials requiring frequent cleaning

Pharmaceutical Applications — Key Requirements

Active pharmaceutical chemicals are typically toxic — purity of production is paramount. Construction must meet cGMP with full validation & documentary support.
  • Manufacture & assembly to highest standards or per User Requirement Specification (URS)
  • All internal contact surfaces cleanable via Clean-In-Place (CIP) — including shaft sealing
  • Zero product trapment within valve
  • Operator Exposure Levels (OEL) within acceptable limits
  • Construction integrity (inc. surface finish) maintained throughout service life
  • All aspects verified by measurement or test
  • Explosion risk secured through full containment; valves must pass explosion & flame containment testing
Flame containment capability requires close operating clearances. FAT inspection requires hot water flush testing (normally 85°C at 20°C ambient) to prove differential expansion during CIP does not cause rotor-body contact.

Pharmaceutical — Typical Design Features

Solid / Coded Weld Fabrication

  • Eliminates castings — no pin-hole porosity or contaminant inclusions
  • Allows quick-release pharma-standard flange connections

Surface Finish

  • Contact surfaces: Ra 0.08 or better, electro-polished (minimises pathogen build-up)
  • External surfaces: Ra 0.5

Precision Manufacture

  • No rotor–bore contact during extraction

Seals

  • FDA-standard seal design
  • No material shedding
  • CIP & drying-in-place capable

Isolator Enclosure (Glove Box)

  • Operable via gloved access, no tools
  • Minimum components (anti-galling materials, no washers)
  • Easy cleaning of external & internal surfaces
  • Easy extraction of rotor/end cover (up to 350 kg) at arm's length

Bearings & Drainage

  • All surfaces self-draining
  • Bearings in sealed housings

Two Types of Abrasion

Type 1 — Mechanical Attrition

Large hard particles / fibrous materials trapped in running clearances

Solutions:

  • Hardfacing — plasma-sprayed ceramics (Tungsten Carbide) or electro-deposited (Hard Chrome, Nickel). Thin coating; once breached ineffective. Suitable for moderately aggressive products only.
  • Replaceable internal contact components — much longer service life due to greater hardness thickness; refurbishable on-site.
  • All-ceramic linings — best abrasion resistance; extremely expensive; must be fully replaced when worn.
Type 2 — High Velocity Erosion

Fine abrasive powders drawn through clearances by leakage air under high pressure differential

  • Primarily an issue above 250 mbar ΔP
  • Velocities can reach 400 m/s through clearances
  • Hard-faced valves rarely suitable for this type
  • Lower pressure differential = lower erosion risk

Product Shearing & Damage

  • On average ~5% of granular product entering valve is subject to shearing between rotor tip & inlet throat
  • Reduction to ~1% achievable using:
    • Offset Inlet design — keeps product away from shear point
    • Inlet Baffle — physical barrier away from shear zone
  • Shearing cannot be totally eliminated
Zero Tolerance / Unshearable Products: Use two valves in tandem — 'Metering / Sealing Assembly'. Top valve fitted with reduced-capacity rotor (clearances > max particle size) discharges in phase with lower valve rotor pockets. Lower valve never fills to point of shear. Takes twice the headroom.

Air Leakage — Overview

  • All rotary valves leak air/gas even with no pressure differential — product entering valve displaces air into inlet area
  • Leakage significantly increases under pressure differential: losses past clearances + carry-over of high-pressure pockets decompressing into inlet
  • Manufacturer leakage charts based on static, product-free tests. Presence of product has mitigating 'Blocking Factor' → published rates provide contingency for blower/fan sizing
  • Where leakage is critical → detailed analysis required taking all duty conditions into account
Upward leakage can significantly impact system performance — positively or negatively — especially under flooded feed conditions.

Effects of Upward Leakage

  • Opposes product flow → reduces throughput
  • Aerates product → reduces bulk density
  • Increases abrasiveness of hard fine-particle products
  • Transfers moisture; creates unstable flow
  • Less problematic as valve size increases — larger throat = better leakage air dispersion
  • Valve should be fitted direct to hopper outlet so leakage air can disperse easily

Mitigation

  • For abrasive products under flood conditions: maintain a head of product above valve to partially suppress leakage — set low-level control high in feed vessel to eliminate internal wear

Venting

  • Required when upward air flow causes unacceptable flow problems
  • Venting is system designer's responsibility — must be allowed for in upstream equipment
  • Difficult to add retrospectively → decide at outset
  • Some manufacturers offer built-in vent dividers in throat — more effective than fabrications; simplifies installation & maintenance removal

Disadvantages of Venting

  • Unvented leakage air can help product flow by fluidising — vent removes this advantage & adds a physical restriction in throat → may require additional flow aids
  • A head of product above valve suppresses leakage (height × permeability × ΔP × bulk density); venting removes this benefit for abrasive products
  • Poorly designed vent compromises system & valve performance
  • Unnecessary venting adds cost & makes cleaning harder (hygiene/contamination applications)
Venting is not always necessary or desirable. Always consider carefully whether to vent and what type to apply.

Feeding Pneumatic Conveying Systems

  • Represents ~50% of rotary valve applications (positive pressure systems)
  • Performance (throughput) affected by pressure differential; pressure differential is itself driven by line loading (throughput) — creates cyclic, erratic behaviour if system has design/construction weaknesses
  • Valve sits at interface between inlet feed system and conveying system → often blamed for poor throughput or high wear; cause usually lies elsewhere in system

ATEX 94/9/EC Overview

  • Directive imposes responsibility on suppliers to guarantee integrity of supply to defined standards
  • Has both procedural and design consequences depending on category of use
  • Most food & pharmaceutical dust forms are potentially explosive → ATEX applies

Category 1 — Explosion / Flame Containment (Autonomous Protective System)

ATEX mandates: if explosion risk exists inside equipment, no ignition must transfer to any external zoned atmosphere. Rotary Valves classified as 'Autonomous Protective System'.

Certification Requirements

  • Each valve must have an EC-Type Examination Certificate from a Notified Body
  • Note: Explosion/Flame Containment certificate from a test house alone does NOT satisfy ATEX — does not prove material or manufacturing integrity of individual valve supplied
  • Notified Body (not manufacturer) responsible for having tests conducted
  • Certificate identifies limits: dust class, internal clearances, vane thickness, pressure limits etc.
  • Hydrostatic pressure testing of each valve required in production — especially critical when using castings
  • Manufacturer's Notified Body must sight & approve technical files
  • Manufacturer must maintain an approved Quality Assurance & Manufacturing system audited by Notified Body
  • Limited use of replaceable components (scraper blades, knife blades) may be allowed — must be supported by adequate fixed vanes to maintain containment; must be approved & certified
  • End user must regularly check clearances remain within certified limits — especially important for abrasive products
  • At elevated temperatures: ensure cold clearances do not exceed certified limits

Categories 2 & 3 — Zone 21 & 22 Dust

  • Rotary Valves generally do not present ignition risk if material selection is careful and rotor tip speed does not exceed 1 m/s
  • Virtually all normal applications result in speeds below 1 m/s
  • Variable speed drives: take care that valve cannot inadvertently exceed 1 m/s limit
  • Manufacturers carry out own risk assessment, establish technical files, and produce IOM instructions to satisfy directive

Zone 20 — External Atmosphere

  • Basic valve does not pose hazard
  • Drive motors must be mounted external to the atmosphere
  • All Zone 20 equipment must be certified by Notified Body who approves: design, technical file, IOM instructions, manufacturing procedures

Internal Atmospheres (Valve NOT Used as Autonomous Safety System)

  • Internal zoning is almost invariably Zone 20 regardless of safety systems
  • EC Type Examination Certificate required: confirms valve does not provide ignition source and can withstand any explosion pressure present
  • Where internal pressure >2 barg: hydrostatic testing to agreed level required
  • Replaceable/adjustable blades normally allowed (valve is not performing flame containment)
Above is guidance only. Responsibility for conforming to current legislation rests with individual manufacturer.
